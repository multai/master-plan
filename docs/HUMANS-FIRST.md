# Humans First

> *The universe is vast. We're just getting started.*

---

## The Question

Should players be able to play as any species, or only as humans?

---

## Recommendation: Humans Only (Initially)

### The Setting

**Earth, ~2400 CE**

Humanity has:
- Achieved faster-than-light travel (recently)
- Established colonies in nearby star systems
- Made first contact with alien civilizations
- Joined (or is joining) the galactic community

We are the **newcomers**. The universe has been here for billions of years. Ancient civilizations have risen and fallen. And we just showed up.

---

## Why Humans?

### 1. Identification

It's easier for players to step into a human's shoes. The wonder of exploring alien worlds, meeting alien species, discovering ancient mysteries — that's amplified when you're experiencing it as a human.

When an alien is truly *alien*, there's tension, mystery, uncertainty:
- "Can I trust this Kelthari merchant?"
- "What do the Machine Collective actually want?"
- "Why is this species acting so strangely?"

If everyone can be any species, aliens become just "humans with different skins."

### 2. Narrative Cohesion

**The Human Story:**
- Newcomers in a galaxy that's been active for millennia
- Learning the rules, making alliances, making enemies
- Proving ourselves (or failing to)
- A natural underdog arc

This is a powerful narrative foundation. Mass Effect, Star Trek, Babylon 5 — all use this effectively.

### 3. Design Simplicity

Balancing multiple playable species is hard:
- Each needs unique abilities, limitations
- Cultural context players must understand
- Different starting conditions
- Potential for "optimal race" meta-gaming

With humans as the only playable species:
- One set of baseline stats
- Alien species are consistently AI-controlled
- Lore stays mysterious (you learn about aliens by interacting, not by being them)

### 4. Alien Mystique

If aliens are only NPCs:
- They can be truly strange — not limited by "what would a player do?"
- Hive minds, silicon-based life, energy beings — all possible
- Their behavior can be unpredictable, their motives unclear
- First contact moments are genuinely alien

## The Human Starting Point

### Earth in 2400

```yaml
setting:
  year: "~2400 CE"
  technology_level: 4-5
  
  achievements:
    - FTL travel (fold drive, 50 years old)
    - Colonies in 12 star systems
    - First contact with 3 alien civilizations
    - United Earth Government (fractious but functional)
    
  challenges:
    - Still learning galactic politics
    - Technologically behind older species
    - Cultural shock — adjusting to not being alone
    - Internal divisions (pro-alien vs isolationist)
    
  status_in_galaxy:
    - "Minor species" — not taken seriously yet
    - Some see potential, others see threat
    - Valuable as neutral parties (no old grudges)
```

### Player Starting Locations

Humans can start in:

| Location | Description | Difficulty |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| **Earth** | Home. Safe, regulated, familiar. | Easy |
| **Colony Worlds** | Human settlements. Some frontier danger. | Medium |
| **Alien Stations** | Human quarters in alien hubs. Fish out of water. | Medium |
| **Deep Space** | Ships, outposts. Far from help. | Hard |
| **Alien Worlds** | Living among non-humans. Rare, challenging. | Very Hard |

## Future Expansion

### Phase 2: Allied Species

After launch, once the core game is stable:

- **Allied species become playable**
- Requires completing certain human storylines first
- "You've earned the trust of the Kelthari. Play as one?"
- Limited selection — close allies only

### Phase 3: Any Species

Far future, if desired:

- Open species selection
- "Veteran mode" for experienced players
- Full alien perspective on the universe

---

## Alternative: "Human Plus"

A middle ground if pure human feels too limiting:

### Variants

Players are human but can be:

| Variant | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Baseline** | Standard human, no modifications |
| **Augmented** | Cybernetic enhancements |
| **Adapted** | Genetically modified for specific environments |
| **Hybrid** | Partial alien DNA (rare, controversial) |
| **Uploaded** | Consciousness in synthetic body |

This gives variety while keeping the "human experience" core.

## The Experience

### Playing as Human

> You dock at Keltar Station for the first time. The air smells wrong — too sweet, metallic undertones. The Kelthari tower over you, their four eyes tracking your movements. Their language is a series of clicks and hums; your translator struggles with idioms.
>
> A merchant approaches. Its posture is... friendly? Aggressive? You can't tell. Human body language doesn't apply here.
>
> "Human," it says through the translator. "You are far from your warm little world. What do you seek in the Dominion?"
>
> Every interaction is a puzzle. Every species, a mystery to unravel.

This experience is harder to achieve if you could just play as a Kelthari.

---

## Summary

| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| **Any species** | Freedom, variety | Complexity, less alien mystique |
| **Humans only** | Strong narrative, simpler design, true alien otherness | Less initial variety |
| **Humans + variants** | Balance of both | Still limited species selection |
| **Humans first, expand later** | Best of both over time | Players must wait for alien gameplay |

**Recommendation:** Start with **humans only** (with optional variants). Expand to allied species in later phases based on player demand and story progression.
